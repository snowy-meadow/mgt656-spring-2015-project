Business Intelligence Questions:

95% of the visitors to our site were new. Below are the answers to the questions as well as screen shots of the Google Analytics tests.

1. How your traffic breaks down across different segments and how they flow through your "funnel" from landing to donation.

During the time period, we had 224 visitors to the site with 86% dropping off at the “Home” page. For those remaining their first interactions breakdown as follows:

  • 72% “House Party” page
  • 22% “About” page
  • 3% “All Events” page
  • 3% “Create New Event” page

Those visitors whose first interaction was the “House Party” page, 52% dropped off and the remaining 42% had a second interaction with the site as follows:

  • 82% returned to the “Home” page
  • 18% visited the “Donation” page

Those visitors whose first interaction was the “About” page, 29% dropped off and the remaining 71% had a second interaction with the site as follows:

  • 60% returned to the “Home” page
  • 40% visited the “House Party” page – of those, 50% donated and 50% dropped off

2. If making the event links on the home page include the word "RSVP" would increase click-through rate.

To maximize the number of people who RSVP for our events, we tested the language used on our RSVP links. Our hypothesis was that adding “RSVP” to the event link would increase the click-through rate as it provides a clearer call-to-action for the visitor. We A/B tested the this “RSVP” link with the normal “non-RSVP” link, measuring click-through-rates for each. The results are as follows:

  • 4.97% conversion rate for the “non-RSVP” link
  • 5.28% conversion rate for the “RSVP” link

The results of the test validated our hypothesis that the “RSVP” would have a better click-through rate.

3. If they should ask people to "donate" or "support" an event in the the donation link text to increase conversions.

Beyond maximizing RSVP, we also want to motivate visitors to donate. We hypothesized that the word “support” would motivate more people to donate than the “donate” button because the word “support” engenders more of a sense of being a part of a community than “donate”. Our hypothesis was validated by an A/B test, with the following results:

  • 5.71% conversion rate for “Donate”
  • 18.18% conversion rate for “Support”

Our hypothesis was validated by the test with “support” generating over a 3X conversion rate compared to “donate”.
